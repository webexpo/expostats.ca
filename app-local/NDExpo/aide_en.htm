Introduction

NDexpo is a JavaScript Web application that implements the robust ROS (regression on order statistics) censored data treatment approach proposed by Denis Helsel.  Details of his approach can be located in his monograph: Nondetects and data analysis: Statistics for censored environmental data. Wiley, 2005.

Basically, the user enters their data values, inserting ‘<x’ for each nondetect. The application implements robust ROS and recreates the data set, replacing ‘<x’ values with exposure estimates based on their rank among the set of detected values. The new data set can then be exported for additional uses such as estimating the geometric mean, the proportion of exposures exceeding an exposure limit, or upper tolerance limits.

 

A note on LODs, LOQs and their uses in NDexpo

In NDexpo, nondetects are entered as ‘<x’ by the user. The value x should be the limit of quantification (LOQ) for the sample, expressed in concentration in the workplace (e.g. ppm).

There is a clear distinction between limit of detection (LOD) and limit of quantification. In brief, the limit of detection (LOD) is a statistical construct aimed at being very sure ‘there was something’. It is usually defined as the product of the variability of blank results by a predetermined factor (typically around 3). The limit of quantification is the limit above which labs feel confident about the numbers they provide.

In theory, it is important to distinguish between data reported as not detected compared to data reported as detected but not quantified. However, to our knowledge very few, if any approach exist to distinguish between ‘<LOD’ and ‘detected but <LOQ’ samples when analysing datasets containing such samples (see section 6.3.4 of the 2012 edition of Helsel's monograph for an exception). Furthermore, labs typically only report results as detected (they report a number) or <LOQ. Consequently, in practice, what we call censored data in occupational hygiene is most often 'not quantified' data rather than 'not detected' data. We will therefore use only the LOQ in the rest of this document.

Many laboratories report the LOQ (or LOD) in mass (e.g. <5ng on the filter). It is important to note that censored data in NDexpo has to be expressed in concentration unit, therefore, LOQs reported in mass have to be combined with sampling volume before entering them in NDexpo in units of concentration on the workplace atmosphere.

 

Software Instructions

Data can be entered in 2 ways: 1) individually in the ‘Raw data’ column (after the value is entered, one presses ‘Add row’ or ENTER); 2) by copy-pasting a column of values from EXCEL in the ‘import’ tab, then clicking on the ‘import’ button. In both cases the status of nondetect is expressed by using the ‘<’ sign (e.g. ‘<10’).

Preparing data

·         Open your spreadsheet

·         Calculate the limit of quantification for each nondetect measurement in unit of concentration in the workplace.

·         In your database add a ‘<’ sign in front of your calculated limit of quantification for each measurement (e.g. ‘<2’).

Enter data 

Data can be entered two ways:

1.       Imputing data by hand.

·         Click on empty box under raw data heading. 

·         Enter a prepared data value and press enter; a new empty row will appear. 

 

2.       Copying data from a spreadsheet program.

·         Select the desired data values from your spreadsheet program and copy data (Note: you can copy over only one column of data at a time). 

·         Click on the import tab and paste the values by either: using CTL- v key functions, or right clicking your mouse in the import area and select paste. 

·         Once data is in import area, click on the ‘Import’ button below your data.  Your data will appear in the main table.

Note: Any value entered in improper format will be highlighted in yellow

Calculate new data base:

·         Once data has been imported into the program (on left side of screen in the main table) click on the ‘Calculate’ button.

·         If there are no errors the program will calculate estimates for the nondetects and display the results in the main table.

Extract/export data.  The program will display and produce three types of results including:

1.       Export (Final only)

·         Click on the ‘Export (final only)’ tab.  This contains the detected and estimated values calculated by the program.

·         To export: Click inside the text area where the exported data is displayed, select all the data (CTL-A or right click and ‘Select all’), copy the data into the clipboard ( CTL-C or right click ‘Copy’ ).The data can be imported/pasted into any spreadsheet or word processing program using the CTL-v/mouse functions.

As an example, the ‘final only’ export tab can be copy-pasted directly in IHSTAT[1] where a variety of lognormal parameters are estimated along with confidence intervals.

 

2.       Export (Verbose)

Click on the Export (verbose). This contains four columns of data including:  raw data ('raw'), plotting positions ('pp', see below), normal scores ('ns', value of the standard normal distribution corresponding to the plotting position), and final values ('final').

The export tabs contain text in tsv format: tab separated values. This implies that when copy/pasting their content (as for the previous case) in EXCEL, the data should appear in separate columns in most cases but exceptions could occur depending on the browser or the spreadsheet settings . Imputed values (i.e. nondetects) and calculated numbers are reported with high precision (i.e. has a lot of digits) in the export tab in order be transferred into other software. On the other hand, the precision of data points entered manually remains as entered by the user. This is why the appearance of data in these tabs might seem inconsistent and difficult to read at times; they are really meant to be exported and displayed elsewhere (e.g. EXCEL).

 

3.       Quantile-Quantile plot (Q-Q plot).  A Q-Q plot allows visual assessment of the goodness of fit of the detected data to a lognormal distribution. It identifies the estimated/imputed nondetects (brown circles) from the detected values (red x’s), and provides the actual concentration value of individual points.  To see individual concentration values, hover over the points on the graph.  Concentrations will be displayed below the graph. We voluntarily did not include a formal goodness of fit test, such as Shapiro-Francia, because these tests tend to be overly severe for large samples (any slight deviation will cause rejection of null hypothesis of log-normality) and overly lenient (null hypothesis never rejected) for small samples. Our main assumption is that there is ample previous evidence that workplace exposures are lognormal, the graph will nevertheless permit to identify gross deviations and outliers.

Adding / replacing values

It is possible to add or eliminate one value and re-do the calculations, which will generate a new final table. Simply fill the empty cell below the last value and press ENTER to add a value. To eliminate a value, erase its content, then click on ‘Remove empty rows’. Re-generate results by clicking on ‘Calculate’.

 

Limitations / troubleshooting

There are some restrictions to the application:

1.       A minimum of 5 measurements is required.

2.       A minimum of 3 detected measurements is required

3.       At least 20% of measurements must be detected values.

4.       Nondetects values must be less than the greatest detected value

 If any of the previous condition (i.e. 1-4) is not met, an error message is given to the effect that calculation is impossible.

There is no limit to the number of values entered in NDexpo. Hence the ROS approach can theoretically be applied to any number of measurements. However, we expect that above 200 measurements (depending on the user’s computer specifications), delays in the calculations might start to appear.

 

Basic principle of the Robust ROS approach

Robust ROS stands for robust regression on order statistics, and is described in detail in section 6.4 of the second edition of Helsel's monograph.

Principle of a Q-Q plot

The basic principle behind robust ROS is the same as that of a quantile-quantile plot (also available in NDexpo). The idea is to compare the observed values to the values we would have observed if the sample came from a known distribution. With the normal distribution, we compare the observed data with data from a normal distribution with the same standard deviation and mean.

The tricky part is to determine which value of the assumed normal distribution each point should be compared to.  For example, assume we have five observations that range from 93 to 108, with a mean of 100 and a standard deviation of 5.  To what value of the corresponding normal distribution should I compare the minimum of my sample?  This has to be repeated for all five observations. To address this we calculate ‘plotting positions’. 

Plotting positions could be seen as the expected cumulative probability corresponding to each observation after they have been ordered. For example the minimum of 5 observations of a normal distribution would be expected to correspond to a cumulative probability of 12% (i.e. 12% of the values in this distribution are smaller than the value, and 88% greater). This probability corresponds to -1.17 in the standard normal distribution (i.e. -1.17 is the normal score corresponding to the cumulative probability 12%). It follows that for a normal distribution of mean 100 and standard deviation 5, a cumulative probability of 12% would correspond to 94 (100-5*1.17). There we go! We have a value to compare to our minimum of 93 !

In order to complete the Q-Q plot, we have to estimate a plotting position for each value of our sample, then calculate the corresponding normal score and the value in the assumed distribution. A Q-Q plot is simply a plot of the observed vs. expected values.

Historically, it has also been popular to calculate the best regression line directly between the observations and their normal scores (this is the type of Q-Q plot displayed in NDexpo). The goodness of fit indicates whether the normal model is plausible, and the slope and intercept provide estimates of the mean and standard deviation.

There are several formulas used to calculate the plotting positions for ranked observations. They usually depend on the assumed distribution. We have used Blom's formula in our example (Royston, 1993). Figure 1 illustrates the process of making a Q-Q plot.

 

 From the Q-Q plot to Robust ROS

The principle of robust ROS is really simple once the principle of the Q-Q plot is secured (see figure 2). The first step is to order the observations. Nondetects are inserted among the detected values depending on the value of the corresponding LOQ (i.e. the x in ‘<x’). Then, plotting positions are estimated for each observation, and normal scores are calculated. As shown between step 1 and 2 in figure 2, there is a re-ordering that occurs after the plotting positions have been calculated. The next step is to perform the regression of detected values on their normal scores. Based on the obtained regression line, a new value is predicted for each nondetect based on its plotting position. The procedure therefore yields a modified dataset, containing the initial detected observations as well as new values replacing the nondetects. The process is illustrated in figure 2.

 

The new dataset can then be processed just as any dataset, allowing it to be used for any statistical procedure (goodness of fit test, estimation of percentiles, confidence intervals, etc.) with no censored data.   It is important to note that the individual imputed values have no meaning, they can only be interpreted within the full dataset. It is especially apparent when there are several data points with the same LOQ; the procedure will yield different imputed values, and there is no way to know which value should be attributed to each point below this LOQ.

The main challenge in applying robust ROS lies in the estimation of plotting positions from the ordered observations. This is especially true when there are multiple censoring points. In his monograph, Dennis Helsel summarized the papers that proposed how to perform this estimation (he was co-author in several of them), and described the resulting recommendation, which was implemented in several statistical software programs including R and Minitab, and now in NDexpo.

Robust ROS for lognormal data

In order to simplify the previous explanation, we have used the normal distribution to illustrate the procedure. Of course, there is now ample evidence that occupational exposures tend to follow the lognormal model. The assumption of lognormality is embedded in NDexpo; after the user has entered the data the application transforms them by calculating the natural logarithm of each value, implements the full ROS procedure as described above, and finally back-transforms the imputed values for the nondetects. It is important to note that the user wishing to use the provided normal scores (in the verbose output) to calculate a regression line manually, would have to take the natural logarithm of the observations first.

 

 

The case of adjacent limits of quantification, and the relationship of NDexpo to the NADA package

Introduction

NDexpo was initially designed to reproduce exactly the procedure described in Helsel's monograph. The earliest computerized implementation of this approach was an add-on for the statistical package R called NADA. Since the second edition of the book, it is also implemented in the statistical package MINITAB. We used extensively the NADA add-on in R to test our code and make sure we got the same results.

There is one set of circumstances however, for which we came to disagree with what we'll refer to as the NADA procedure. Although the imputed value from NDexpo will differ from those from NADA in these circumstances, we expect the impact on the estimation of parameters and statistical procedure should be minimal: the regression line from which the nondetects are imputed is not affected, only their position on the line (plotting positions). To cater for users that might not be convinced by our reasoning (see below), a button on the main NDexpo page permits to switch to what we called the 'NADA mode’. In this mode, the calculations made by NDexpo are identical to the NADA procedure as implemented in R. We intend in the near future to perform simulations to evaluate the actual impact of our modification and assess whether it has any clear advantage or drawback.

The proposed modification

The issue arises when more than one censored value with different LOQs end up adjacent after the data are ordered (see example in figure 3). As can be seen in figure 3, we have two groups of nondetects, 3 points censored at 1.5, and 3 points censored at 1. Because of the adjacency of the 2 groups (i.e. there are no detected values between the two LOQs), the estimation of their plotting positions is such that for both groups, the 'probability space' between the LOQ and 0 is divided in the same way. This leads to the plotting positions in the 'NADA' column of figure 3, where there are 3 pairs of equal values, which would translate into 3 pairs of equal imputed values. We believe this is counter intuitive. In that case we believe there should be 6 different plotting positions, and the 6 observations should be considered as a single group of censored data (see figure 4): the 'probability space' between the LOQ and 0 should be divided by 6 once instead of being divided twice by three. The fact that there are 2 censoring levels (1.5 and 1) does not come into play because after the data is ranked, the actual LOQ values are not used anymore. In our opinion this represents an additional justification for lumping the 2 groups of data. One way to obtain this result in NADA is to replace the 3 points <1.5 and 3 points <1 by 6 points either <1 or <1.5. Of course, at the individual level, <1 implies something different from <1.5. However, within the robust ROS procedure in the situation that there are no detected value between 1.5 and 1, either value can be used as the new ‘group LOQ': it has no impact whatsoever. This trick is precisely what NDexpo does whenever a situation of adjacent LOQs is detected. After having ordered the data, a new dataset is created where all adjacent LOQs are set at the same value (we chose the highest, but underline that it has no bearing on the results), and the NADA procedure is applied to this new dataset. This is all blind to the user.

The situation appears more acute if we imagine a set of 10 censored values, with the same analytical LOQ in mass, but slightly different but adjacent LOQs in concentrations because of slightly variable sampling volumes.  In this scenario the NADA procedure would end up with 10 equal plotting positions, and therefore the same imputed value for the 10 censoring points.

In the case where the adjacent groups of censored points have different sizes (e.g. 3 points <1.0 and 6 points <1.5), the issue is more difficult to detect because the plotting positions would not end up equal; the 'probability space' would be divided by 3, and then by 6 to calculate them. However we still believe it would be better divided by 9.

We presented acute examples in order to justify our approach. However, we do not believe the circumstances of adjacent LOQs will occur very frequently and therefore believe our results would identical to the NADA procedure in many situations. Furthermore, as stated earlier, the impact on parameter estimates should be small.

 

 

Potential inconsistencies in the results

It is likely that at some point the user might face a situation where the imputed value for a censored point ends up greater than the entered LOQ, even greater than the next detected value. This situation is related to the goodness of fit of the data to the assumed distribution (in our case lognormal). Hence, the procedure will ensure that the plotting positions of the nondetects are consistent with their rank in the dataset: the plotting position of a nondetect will always be smaller than that of the next lowest detected value. Consequently their value in the assumed distribution will also be consistent. However, if the dataset does not fit the lognormal distribution well, it is possible that the real value of a detected point could be low compared to its ‘ideal value’ (i.e. the value corresponding to its plotting position on the regression line). If the departure from lognormality is severe enough, the observed value can be lower than the predicted value for an adjacent nondetect (see figure 5). Therefore, it must be clear that this kind of observation has no relationship to the accuracy / performance of the robust ROS procedure, only to the actual fit of the data to the lognormal dist8ribution.
