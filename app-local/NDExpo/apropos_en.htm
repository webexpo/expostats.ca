NDExpo: treatment of censored data with multiple limits of detection

About

 

Latest version: V1.0 (November 2013)

Introduction

NDexpo is a Web application that permits the analysis of measurement data  containing values reported as lower than a limit of quantification (LOQ) or limit of detection (LOD), also called ‘censored data’, or ‘nondetects’.  NDexpo works by replacing the censored data with numerical values estimated according to a recognized methodology described below. The modified dataset can then be used in any statistical procedure aimed at testing hypotheses or estimating parameters such as the geometric mean or percentiles of the underlying distribution.  Details about how to use NDexpo and a description of the approach can be found in the accompanying help section.

Some history

Data reported as not detected or not quantified have been a nuisance to many practitioners for decades. These data cannot be processed by spreadsheet software routinely used by most industrial hygienists. While many felt intuitively that we should not eliminate such data, or replace them by zero or the limit of quantification itself, practical alternatives appear to have been extremely scarce. A paper by Hornung and Reed in 1990 concluded that replacing censored data by any fixed arbitrary value was error prone. Nevertheless they noted that using LOQ/2 or LOQ/  was leading to the least error [1]. Despite the availability of statistical methods able to incorporate information from nondetects as soon as the late fifties, the lack of practical and user-friendly tool have made Hornung and Reed’s recommendation the standard way of dealing with them for the last 25 years.  In the last five years, interest in the treatment of censored data in occupational hygiene has mounted in the literature.  In fact, a total of 6 papers appeared in the Annals of Occupational Hygiene alone [2–7], including a notable editorial on the topic by Trevor Ogden, editor in chief at the time of publication, calling for the development of simple tools [8]. In 2005, what is arguably the bible of censored environmental data analysis was published by Dennis Helsel [9]. His book, for which a second edition was published in 2012, contains a comprehensive review of the issue and summarizes the available methods and the results of simulation studies assessing their performance. The latest edition is accompanied by examples of implementation of some of the approaches with the statistical packages R and Minitab [10]. A notable implementation effort was undertaken by Drs Lee and Helsel, who created the NADA add-on, which implements most of the approaches presented in the monograph in the R environment. 

Development of NDexpo

NDexpo represents the implementation (in a web environment) of the approach presented by Helsel in the NADA package that seems to perform well in a wide variety of conditions, called Robust ROS (regression on order statistics). This approach was also referred to as LPR (log probit regression) by Hewett and Ganser [4]. It is appealing because it outputs a modified dataset that can be processed in the same manner as if it was a dataset with no censored data.

This project was initially started as an effort to implement Robust ROS in EXCEL (which will probably occur in 2014). Early participants involved with the development of the first version of NDexpo included Daniel Drolet (AIHA exposure assessment committee), Jérôme Lavoué (University of Montréal) and André Dufresne (University of Montréal). NDexpo in its current state has been elaborated by Jérome Lavoué and François Lemay, a computer programmer who wrote the javascript code.  It has been financed by personal research funds from Jérôme Lavoué. We are also much grateful to Tracy Kirkham, for her review of the documentation.

Important note: The algorithm used by NDexpo is very close to the robust ROS approach described by Helsel, and NDexpo will yield exactly the same results as the NADA add-on in R in a majority of situations. However there is one particular type of data structure for which there is a slight departure from Helsel’s procedure. This happens in the case of multiples limits of quantification, and only when different LOQs are adjacent after the data have been ordered (e.g. 1, 3, <5, <8, 10, 12, but not for 1, 3, <5, 7, <8, 10, 12). In this case NDexpo will yield slightly different values for these censored data compared to NADA. The impact on estimation is expected to be quite small, but has not been studied yet. The justification for this modification is described and discussed in the accompanying help file. We have programmed the NDexpo web application to allow the user to choose between our proposition and the procedure implemented in the NADA add-on.
